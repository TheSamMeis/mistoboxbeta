Coffee Varieties

"Coffee has a unique benefit of being as simple or complex as you make it. There are a few concepts that will help you understand brewing theory more thoroughly."

Coffee can be classified in various groups. Most commonly, we know that coffee can be distinguished as Arabica or Robusta. These are referring to the species of coffee. However, we can classify coffees even further than just by species. These are what are referred to as varieties. In coffee literature, you will sometimes see them noted as 'botanical variety' or 'cultivar' a word made by combining 'cultivated varieties', but we will simply call them 'varieties'.

You will sometimes see people use the word 'varietal' to mean 'variety' in coffee. â€œVarietal" is actually a wine term, referring to wines being comprised of only one type of grape variety or labeled 'varietally'. In recent years, the coffee industry has adopted this term, but we prefer to leave it to the wine industry and stick with 'variety'.

Just like apples can be classified by their variety, so can coffee. Think about how Granny Smith, Honeycrisp and Pink Lady are all varieties of apples. Each one has certain characteristics that we associate with it. Granny Smith are green and round, medium in size, tart, juicy and great for baking with. Honeycrisp are sweet, larger in size, and have a different texture. While both are apples, you might prefer one over the other.

Think about coffee the same way. Each variety has different genetic traits that can determine how a coffee will taste, how much each coffee each shrub can produce every year, how resistant the plant is to disease, and even how tall the shrub can grow.

Bourbon, Typica, Caturra, Pacamara and Catuai are some of the most common varieties you will see. (Include images of various colors, shapes, sizes with labels)
