| Coffee Grinding |

We use whole bean coffee that is freshly roasted and ideally stored (because it has delicious flavors we want to taste). We have to grind it, so those flavors are easier to get to, and then use hot water to wash them out because hot water is great at dissolving these flavors and trapping flavorful gases

We have to grind the coffee to make these flavors more accessible. Depending on how we are going to extract these flavors, we need to choose a grind size that will get everything we want without getting flavors we don't want, like bitterness or astringency.

We use hot water to wash flavors (flavor particles capable of being dissolved = soluble solids) out of the bean itself.

| Partical Size & Uniformity|

Particle size is extremely important, and one of the important aspect of the grinding process because it dicateats how your coffee will taste. What is the correct particle size? That depends on which method you are brewing.

As a rule of thumb, the larger the grind size, the more contact time with water for extraction and, the smaller the grind, the less time water and grounds should be in contact.

| Burr & Blade Grinders |

Burr and blade grinders - what's the difference?

Burr Grinders

Most home burr grinders are conical. Only one size of particles can fit between the burrs, grinding coffee not only specifically for how you want to brew it, but also giving consistency of size

Blade grinders

Blade grinders are probably the most common type of "grinder" found in homes across the US for one reason: cost. It's not actually a grinder, more of a chopper. Burrs grind the coffee by passing between two plates that creates a milling action, blade grinders use a blades connected to a using high rotation frequencies to rapidly chop the beans into smaller particle sizes

Pros vs. Cons of Burrs

Pros: consistent, even, specific grind settings, control, delivers desired results, less friction, less heat from motors.

Cons: more expensive

Pros vs. Cons of Blades

Pros: cost effective, easy to find

Cons: friction, grinding compartment located above motor, blade uses chopping action rather than grinding, inconsistent, uneven, difficult to get consistent results

4 key problems associated with blade grinders

Friction

Rapidly chopping the beans with rotating blades create lots of friction when they come in contact with the beans, creating excess heat, diminishing the freshness

Motor

The grinding compartment usually resides directly above the motor, which gives off a good amount of heat. This adds to the heat caused by friction, again diminishing the freshness of the coffee

Unevenness

No matter how long you keep the blade going, you will always have a huge variance of particle size. Too many fines and it will result in an overextracted, bitter brew; too many large coarse pieces, will result in underdeveloped flavors.

Inconsistencey

It's difficult to get consistent results. Even if you use time as the main variable in determining grind size, grinding for a set amount of time every time you brew doesn't necessarily mean you get the same results.
